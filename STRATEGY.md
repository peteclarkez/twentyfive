# Strategy Guide for Twenty-Five (25s)

This document captures established strategic principles and social conventions for 25s.
It is intended to inform both human players and AI player implementations.

---

## The Core Social Contract: Keep the Leader Down

The most important strategic principle in 25s — and a defining cultural feature of the game
for centuries — is the **informal collective effort to stop the leading player**.

While players compete individually, playing in a purely selfish way is contrary to the spirit
of the game. When one player pulls ahead, all others are expected to cooperate to prevent
them from winning, even at personal cost.

**The threshold:** When a player reaches **20 points**, they become the active target. At this
point, other players should prioritise preventing that player from winning tricks over
maximising their own score.

> *"The game was always a fight to prevent other people winning. No wise player ever admitted
> by his style of play that he had any expectation of winning himself."*
> — James Owen Hannay, 1914

## Trump Management

### Hold the Five for the Kill

The Five of trumps is unbeatable. Inexperienced players use it as soon as a good opportunity
arises to guarantee themselves a trick. Expert players hold it in reserve to deploy against
the leading player at the critical moment.

**Example:** You hold 5♠ (trumps) and K♥. You are guaranteed the trick if you play the Five.
The natural instinct is to play the King hoping to win a second trick on the lead. But expert
play says: keep the Five, wait until the leading player (on 20 points) is about to win a trick,
then play the Five to take it from them — even if this costs you the chance at ten points.



### Smoke Out Good Cards with a Trump Lead

If a player is on 20 points going into a trick, the player leading should consider opening
with a trump — even a low one — to force others to reveal their trumps. This is especially
valuable when you suspect the leader holds strong cards they would otherwise hold back.

A player will sacrifice their own good lead (e.g. a King) to play a trump if it forces the
leader to commit their hand. This is expected, not selfish.

### Don't Waste the Five on an Easy Trick

If you can win a trick cheaply (with a King or Jack of non-trump), do so. Reserve the Five
for a trick where it makes a difference — either robbing a near-winner of 5 points, or
blocking someone from reaching 25.

### Holding Trump for Positional Advantage

If you have a weak hand with only one or two strong cards, patience is key. Holding your
best trump back until trick 4 gives you two benefits: you win trick 4, and winning trick 4
puts you in the lead for trick 5 — meaning you get to dictate the suit.

**Example:** Your hand is a mix of low cards, a Jack of trumps, and a Queen of Clubs
(non-trump). Rather than playing the Jack early, bide your time. Play the Jack on trick 4
to win it. Now you lead trick 5 with the Queen of Clubs — you've chosen the suit, so any
player without clubs (or with only weaker clubs) is at a disadvantage. You've turned a weak
hand into a realistic shot at two tricks.

The same logic applies to a strong non-trump card: play it late when you can lead the suit
yourself, rather than early when another player controls the lead.

## The "May Trump" Decision (Prodding)

Playing a trump when a non-trump suit is led is called a **prod**. The decision to prod is
strategic:

**Reasons to prod:**
- You have weak cards in the led suit and want to win the trick
- You want to exhaust an opponent's trumps
- The leading player is likely to win the trick otherwise

**Reasons not to prod:**
- You want to conserve your trumps for a later, more important trick
- Another player has already trumped and you cannot over-trump — discard a weak card instead
- The trick is not worth contesting (no one near 20 points is winning it)

### Prod Early with a Weak Trump

A weak trump (e.g. 2 or 3 of trumps) is best used as a prod early in the hand (trick 1 or 2)
to win a trick it would otherwise lose. Holding it too long is risky — if another player leads
trump, you'll be forced to play it anyway, this time without choosing the moment.

## Reneging Strategically

The renege privilege (withholding the 5, Jack, or A♥ when lower trump leads) is a powerful
defensive tool:

- **Withhold the Five** when trump is led and you intend to use it on a more important trick. You are never forced to commit it unless the Five itself leads.
- **Withhold the Jack** (when A♥ leads) to save it for a later trick where it may decide the game.
- **Renege to deceive:** Holding back your Five when a lower trump leads signals nothing — other players do not know what you have. Use this ambiguity to your advantage.
- **Hold your height back when the leader is already losing the trick.** If a lower trump is led and another player has already played a card that will beat the leader, you don't need to commit your Five or Jack — renege and save it. Use it when the leader is actually winning a trick and needs to be stopped. This is especially effective when sitting immediately after (to the left of) the leading player.

## Robbing: When to Rob and What to Take

You should **almost always rob** if you hold the Ace of trumps. The traded card is face-up
and known — taking it gives you a net improvement and information.

**What to discard when robbing:**
- Discard your weakest card: a low non-trump that is unlikely to win anything
- If you already have strong trumps, consider discarding a middling non-trump to keep  suit balance
- Never discard a card that gives useful information to opponents about your hand

**When the dealer robs the face-up Ace:**
The dealer should almost always take the Ace of trumps if they can. It is the 4th highest trump and worth a discard of any weak card. The only exception is if the dealer has an exceptionally strong hand and prefers the existing trump card — which is rare. They may also not rob if they don't want to show other players they have a hand full of trumps

## Endgame: Someone is on 20 Points

When a player is on 20 points, they need only **one more trick** to win (5 points = 25 total).
This triggers a collective defensive mode:

1. **The lead player should open with trump** to smoke out any strong cards before the  near-winner can use them.
2. **Hold your Five in reserve** until the near-winner is about to win a trick, then play it.
3. **Let another player win the trick** rather than the near-winner — it is better for a
   neutral player to score than to let the leader win.
4. **Don't be greedy:** If you are on 15 points and could take a trick with your King, but
   the near-winner will also play a strong card — consider whether letting a third player
   win the trick is better than contesting it and accidentally letting the near-winner
   through.

## Hand Evaluation

A rough guide to hand strength:

| Hand contains | Assessment |
|---|---|
| 5 of trumps | Strong — you have a guaranteed trick and can stop the leader |
| Jack of trumps + another trump | Good — two likely tricks |
| A♥ + any other trump | Solid — at least one strong trump trick |
| A♥ alone (no other trumps) | Moderate — one trump trick, use wisely |
| Ace of trumps (can rob) | Good — rob to improve your hand, gain 4th-best trump |
| No trumps at all | Weak — rely on high non-trump cards and hope to survive |
| K or Q of trumps only | Below average — beatable by any of the top 3 |

## Social Conventions

These are widely observed informal rules that govern the social game:

- **Never spoil deliberately without cause.** If you can win a trick cleanly, don't throw a worthless card just to waste a trick — unless preventing the leader from winning.
- **Commiserate, don't gloat.** The game has a convivial, talkative tradition. Commentary is expected — silence after a big play is unusual.
- **Robbing is always legal and never unsporting.** Never hesitate to rob if you hold the Ace of trumps.

## Notes for AI Player Implementation

When implementing a robotic player, the following priority order is a reasonable starting
point for decision-making:

1. **If someone is on 20 points:** Prioritise stopping them over all else
2. **If you can win a trick with a non-trump:** Do so, conserve your trumps
3. **If you must trump to win:** Weigh whether the trick is worth using a trump
4. **If you have the Five:** Reserve it unless using it now prevents the leader from winning
5. **If you cannot win the trick:** Play your weakest card (discard)
6. **Rob whenever possible** — almost always correct
